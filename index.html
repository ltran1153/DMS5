Project Speculation

Purpose:

My initial ideation for this project’s creative tool is a text generator that allows users to quickly and easily create pieces of literature based on a couple of chosen words. This function allows users to have an input in the literature they read without the need to think and write from scratch. An example of how this tool could work is the Mad Libs-style stories from thewordfinder.com, which allows users to blindly fill in words into blank spaces of a story, often resulting in nonsensical scenarios. The poem generator from poem-generator.org.uk is an example of a generative tool capable of creating poems from scratch with just a few inputs from the user. This allows users to see their words put into an artistic and evocative piece of literature. Both of these examples show my creative tool’s potential for assisting users with generating a piece of literature without the need for much experience or effort.

Values:

The value of my creative tool is not in the ability to generate literature itself, but rather, as a great source of entertainment, both individually and in a group setting. User input pieces of literature often provide a great source of humor, and the thrill of seeing how one’s input impacted the writing in a social setting is often very enjoyable. The tile flipping toy in ash-k.itch.io/tile-toy shows the value of how one’s small input can turn into a very cool piece of art, and be able to share that cool creation for others to see. My tool aims to mirror that kind of value, but in the context of humor. My type of creative tool is proven to have value by the existence of Mad Libs being widely popular; however, that also means my idea is currently unoriginal and will not provide value over other alternatives. My solution to this problem is to make the tool stand out through its presentation; by gamifying my tool, I will make the audience feel like they are playing with it rather than generating. The inspiration behind this is the Wigglypaint from internet-janitor.itch.io/wigglypaint, a painting software that stands out for its video game-like design and interactions. Very basic digital painting options in this software provide users with playful and stimulating feedback, making drawing here feel fun, whereas it is normally a very meticulous and serious hobby. My experimentation with feedback design also proves this idea; through the concept of gemefying, I was able to make pressing a button give the users a sense of progression and make the action feel more interactive than it should. The value of having a fun and playful tool packaged with a fun and playful presentation is making the experience a video game, not just a software to do a job.

Framing:

For my creative tool’s framing, I am considering the scope and how to use the tool’s constraints to its advantage. This tool is unable to create entire pieces of literature from scratch, therefore I will need to preset several writings that are chosen randomly so that users will get something different for the duration of their use. Additionally, I aim to add a restriction to words by only allowing users to select words from a small list. This restriction can both bolster creativity, providing words that the user may never think of, and add personality that fits the tool’s presentation and aesthetic. Concrete Nest from https://gamesforcrows.itch.io/concrete-nest is my inspiration for how I could make restrictions feel not like a limitation of the tool, but a feature that adds personality to the project. The words in Concrete Nest are varied enough that, for the duration of my visit, I did not see words overlap. Some of the words were long, complex, and flowery, which fits the serene and contemplative atmosphere to make using this tool feel like creating a delicate piece of art. The information design experiment I did is personal experience that restrictions combined with presentation makes the design feel deliberate and creative. The restriction with this project was the alien-sounding keyboard; by creating a space-themed design around that concept, the restriction now becomes more purposeful and enhances the overall experiment. I believe this concept will translate well through my gamefied tool, making users feel like they are a character inside the video game. Framing the tool within certain mechanical constraints will change the way users perceive and interact with this project. My experiment with mapping design showed me that the control of tools can change how a user interacts with and perceives a project. A tool that allows users to click a few buttons to reach the desired result will be seen as simple and automated, while a tool that gives users a wide range of potential and many input options will be seen as complex and manual. The aim for my creative tool is to strike a balance between simple and complex; I want my tool to provide users with desired results without much thinking and effort required, however, not automate the process so much that the users feel like their input has little value. I think that, with correct framing, restrictions can feel like creative choices, while interactions can feel engaging and intuitive.

Development and Prototyping

Conceptual Response:

Since my project speculation, I have chosen to finalize my creative context from a previously abstract literature generator into a concrete mad-libs styled project that allows users to influence the stories that they are reading. I have taken three key ideas from my project speculation: humor, gamification (through narrative, aesthetic, and design), and the exploration of new words, and made them the main principles to build my project on. These principles combined make my project unique, adding originality to all three interaction design principles of information design, feedback, and mapping.
My project’s information design stands out for its simplicity and aesthetic. All information in this project will fit in one display and immediately stand out, which should allow users to experiment and understand the functions of each interaction quickly. The aesthetic of my design is based on a narrative of a wandering bard who travels across different regions to perform. This aesthetic choice provides a unique context and visuals that other mad-libs-style tools don’t have, and it also makes it easier for users to grasp the purpose of this tool. By providing context that the user is playing as a wandering bard telling stories, the users will understand why they are selecting words and filling a passage with random words.
The feedback design of my project aims to immerse the users in the narrative context of my project, as well as provide humor. By creating tavern/ fantasy themed feedback through areas such as sound and visuals, users should be more immersed in the narrative, controlling the outcome of the story, and the music the bard will play, as well as the bard’s reactions. A part of the humor in this project comes from the mad-libs story; however, by itself, the story is neither very funny nor original. A large part of this project’s humor comes from the way the mad-libs is framed in the context of a bard improvising a story to tell their audience. The mad-libs creation, combined with the bard reactions and music, is what makes this project’s humor stand out.
The mapping design for this project is guided by its ease of use, as well as helping users discover or use words that are very uncommon in their vocabulary. There is very little complexity in my project’s interactions that would require users to spend a lot of time or put any effort into use at its full capacity, as everything is designed to be a single click. By creating a list of words for the users to choose from, instead of letting users pick their own words, I removed the need to think of words, therefore users can quickly and effortlessly generate humorous mad-libs in a few clicks. Additionally, by creating a preset list of words and filling it with weird and uncommon words, it allows users to see and test new words that they might have never used before, likely enhancing the comedy, as well as introducing a word to their vocabulary.
Overall, by adding some constraints and applying the tool to a very unique context, I believe I was able to create something purposeful and original.

Technical Response:

I believe that the main coding for this project will come down to two elements: a list and a randomizer.
To create a list, I can use a JavaScript array to add all of my words into a list and create functions based on that list. From reading the many possibilities of arrays from www.w3schools.com/jsref/jsref_obj_array.asp, there are a few pieces of code that stick out. The forEach() code treats every item in an array as separate and applies any functions I write to all items inside the array individually. This will be very important as it will allow me to create a large quantity of items all at once. The other code I find crucial is slice(). This code allows me to select a specific item or a number of items from my array instead of the entire list. This is important because when combined with a randomizer, the item that slice() selects will be random, creating a different list of words for the users to see each time.
Randomness is created by the math.random() function in JavaScript. By applying the math.random() function to things such as scale or position, it will randomize their values and provide a different result each time an element loads. This will be useful, as randomly placing words within an area is a key concept in my project. Although math.random() is useful by itself for many applications, it is unable to properly shuffle an array. To shuffle an array, we have to apply math.random() through a specific algorithm called the Fisher-Yates shuffle. This is a well-known algorithm for many web developers, with the website where I learned and took the algorithm from being dev.to/tanvir_azad/fisher-yates-shuffle-the-right-way-to-randomize-an-array-4d2p. This shuffle is considered to be one of the best pseudo-random methods for developers to use, and the method I will be using for randomizing my array.
To materialize the words inside my array into words that users can see. I will be using the Konva-Template extension. By using the Konva template, I can create an area where all of my words will be focused, as well as separate different aspects of my tool into layers, such as adjectives, verbs, nouns, and story. By doing this, I can control each layer and its contents separately, such as revealing and hiding certain layers when conditions are met. Using Konva.Text, I can turn the words inside my array into text and stylize it all inside of JavaScript instead of having to create elements in HTML and using CSS to style.
Other than the creation of core elements, I can add extra feedback to assist the main content, such as hover effects (:hover, mouseover), as well as importing audio that plays when the user hovers or clicks objects.
Overall, I have an idea of what types of functions I will be using, but am not yet sure how implementing these functions would be like.

Peer Reflection

Through the peer review responses, I was able to obtain insights into my conceptual and technical application of the project that will be crucial for my development moving forward.
My conceptual application of the project was overall very well received. I found that my peers unanimously agreed that my framing and application of “gamification” made the project stand out as something original. The characterization and atmosphere created by my project’s aesthetics and music seems to be the overall consensus for why my project stood out, with some outlier responses stating the story and randomness being a strong reason why it was unique. The story generation loop of the project was overall well received with most responses state wanting/somewhat wanting to see what different seeds can be generated. When reading the recommendations for what could be developed further, moving forward was for the story to be longer. This makes me believe that the story is currently the weakest application of my concept and will need to be focused on a lot more as I progress. Other suggestions relate to variety when it comes to the project’s visuals and audio, with some peers wanting more customization, while others suggest random audio and visual assets. Some unique suggestions state that I should add a word input option or an undo feature; however, I believe these options go against my design principle. Moving forward, I have to think about whether I want users to have more customization over audio and visuals, or have it be a randomized feature. Providing users with customization will keep them more engaged via satisfaction, while randomness will keep users more engaged via experimentation, as well as fit more with the overall concept of the project.
My technical application of this project requires work when it comes to information design. The text on my project right now is considered difficult to read for a large number of users, which is primarily due to the type of font I use, but also because of my color choices. The other issue that a few users are encountering is related to the word containers. Each user had a different issue with the containers being: size, position, style, and color; therefore, fixing the containers will be about striking a balance with the design to be as visually clear as possible. I might also tune down the volume of my ambient music, although only 1 user seems to have an issue with it, I don’t think there will be any issue at large with tuning it down. There was no overall issue with any users when it came to understanding how to use the tool, despite not having any instructions directing users on what to do. This shows that my project’s layout and simplicity worked quite well. The project is also mostly rid of bugs, with most responses stating that they did not notice any malfunctions. However, there are two responses stating that my ambient button wasn’t working. There wasn’t an explanation for the reasoning, but since I never encountered the bug myself, I believe it could be because the ambient does not apply to guitar music, or that the user did not have sound on and mistook my meaning with “ambient”. A bug that none of the users noted is that the position of the bard is sometimes misplaced on different displays; this could be because the users didn’t see or recognize it as a bug, but it is something that I intend to fix moving forward.
My overall sentiment from reading the peer reviews for my prototype is that my project is headed in the right direction, and my development moving forward is defined and clear. I need to focus on evolving my project’s strongest assets, being immersion through audio and visual design, while keeping in mind information design to maintain good visual clarity through many design variations.
